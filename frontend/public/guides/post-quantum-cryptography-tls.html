Post-quantum cryptography for TLS: ML-DSA, SLH-DSA & Falcon

In 2024 NIST finalized the first post-quantum cryptography (PQC) standards. Here's what they are, what they replace, and how to start using post-quantum signatures in certificates today.

Why now

Classical public-key algorithms (RSA, ECDSA) fall to Shor's algorithm on a large quantum computer. To get ahead of "harvest now, decrypt later," NIST ran a multi-year competition and standardized replacements built on quantum-hard math (lattices and hashes).

The signature standards

StandardNameBasisNotes
FIPS 204ML-DSA (was CRYSTALS-Dilithium)LatticeThe primary, general-purpose choice. Sets: ML-DSA-44/65/87.
FIPS 205SLH-DSA (was SPHINCS+)HashConservative, stateless, larger/slower signatures. Great when you distrust lattices.
DraftFalcon (FN-DSA)Lattice (NTRU)Very compact signatures; more complex to implement safely.

(For key encapsulation — the other half of TLS — the standard is ML-KEM, FIPS 203. This guide focuses on signatures, which is what CSRs and certificates use.)

Which parameter set?

  • ML-DSA-65 — the sensible default; NIST security level 3.
  • ML-DSA-44 — smaller/faster, level 2.
  • ML-DSA-87 — strongest, level 5.
  • SLH-DSA — choose if you want hash-based conservatism and can accept bigger signatures.

The catch: adoption

PQC signatures are standardized, but public CAs and browsers don't broadly issue or trust PQC certificates yet, and signatures are kilobytes (vs bytes classically), which affects handshake size. So today PQC shines for:

  • Internal CAs and private PKI you control.
  • Testing and pilots to measure size/performance impact.
  • Crypto-agility: proving your pipeline can produce and handle PQC before you're forced to.
